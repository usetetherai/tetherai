Explore Abuse Types and Abuser Profiles

Abuse Type: Intimate Terrorism

Intimate Terrorism involves a sustained pattern of control, intimidation, and often physical violence. Victims typically experience ongoing fear and trauma. Formal supports like shelters or DV programs improve outcomes for over 70% of victims.

Abuse Type: Situational Couple Violence

Situational Couple Violence is conflict-driven and less about control. It typically arises from escalating arguments rather than intentional coercion. It is associated with lower fear and less severe outcomes.

Abuse Type: Nonviolent Coercive Control

Nonviolent Coercive Control involves strict control through manipulation, monitoring, or emotional pressure without overt physical violence. Victims still report significant fear and loss of autonomy.

Abuser Profile: Controlling

Controlling abusers use long-term strategies of dominance, surveillance, and emotional suppression. Their behavior is often planned, persistent, and escalates when challenged.

Abuser Profile: Reactive

Reactive abusers explode in response to perceived threats or slights. Their violence may be intense but less premeditated, often escalating situationally. Victim safety can be improved by de-escalation and external accountability structures.

Abuser Profile: Calculating

Calculating abusers are strategic, using silence, guilt, and manipulation. They often appear calm or 'reasonable' publicly, masking private cruelty. Their abuse may evade detection but leaves deep psychological harm.

Abuser Profile: Narcissistic

Narcissistic abusers centralize their own needs, use blame-shifting and emotional punishment, and react poorly to boundaries. Their need for control and admiration can make separation especially risky for the victim.
